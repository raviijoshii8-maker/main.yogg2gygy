The Living Tradition

Yoga is an ancient practice that unites body, breath, and awareness. The Sanskrit root yuj means "to yoke" or "to join" — bringing together effort and ease, movement and stillness. It's not a workout but a homecoming.

In the modern world, yoga often arrives as asana — the physical postures. Yet classical yoga (Patanjali's Yoga Sūtras) outlines eight limbs: ethical precepts, breath, sense withdrawal, concentration, meditation, and more. Asana is just one step.

  • • Yamas & niyamas — ethical guidelines
  • • Pranayama — breath as life force
  • • Dhyana — meditation, the art of observing

Why We Practice — Ten Uses

  1. To inhabit the body fully — most of us live in our heads; yoga draws attention back to physical sensations.
  2. To regulate the nervous system — conscious breathing shifts us from fight‑or‑flight to rest‑and‑digest.
  3. To cultivate concentration — holding a pose trains the mind to focus.
  4. To build functional strength & mobility — for ease: carrying groceries, playing with kids.
  5. To process emotions — hips and shoulders store tension; mindful movement can release it.
  6. To find community — group practice offers quiet connection without performance pressure.
  7. To improve sleep — restorative postures and breathwork calm the mind.
  8. To support recovery — yoga adapted to individual needs aids healing.
  9. To explore self‑inquiry — "who am I beneath the thoughts?"
  10. To simply pause — permission to be still for an hour.

What Research Confirms — Benefits

  • Reduced cortisol: 12 weeks of yoga decreases stress markers.
  • Improved flexibility & balance: critical for healthy aging.
  • Spinal decompression: poses like cat‑cow hydrate spinal discs.
  • Heart health: lowers blood pressure via vagal stimulation.
  • Better sleep: 8 weeks of yoga nidra improves sleep onset.
  • Emotional regulation: thicker prefrontal cortex, reduced amygdala reactivity.
  • Lung capacity: pranayama expands tidal volume.
  • Inflammation reduction: lower IL‑6 levels.

consistency matters more than intensity — 2‑3 sessions a week make a difference.

A Glimpse Beyond Asana — The Eight Limbs

Patanjali's Yoga Sūtras describe an eightfold path. Most modern classes focus on āsana, but the other limbs enrich practice:

  • Yama – ethical standards (non‑violence, truthfulness)
  • Niyama – self‑discipline (purity, contentment, self‑study)
  • Āsana – posture, seat
  • Prāṇāyāma – breath control
  • Pratyāhāra – withdrawal of senses
  • Dhāraṇā – concentration
  • Dhyāna – meditation
  • Samādhi – blissful absorption

Yoga For Every Chapter Of Life

Prenatal & postnatal: gentle asanas to support changing bodies, breathing techniques for labor, and pelvic floor awareness. (We recommend starting second trimester.)

Seniors & limited mobility: chair yoga, wall‑supported poses, emphasis on balance and joint health. No previous experience needed.

Athletes: yoga complements running, cycling, team sports — improves flexibility, prevents injury, and aids recovery.

Stress & burnout: slow flows, extended savasana, and yoga nidra (guided relaxation). Many students arrive exhausted and leave refreshed.
